CHANAKYA NITI AND ARTHASHASTRA — CORE TEACHINGS
================================================

CHANAKYA NITI — BOOK 1: ON EDUCATION AND SELF-DEVELOPMENT

"Vidya viheenah pashuh." — Without education, a man is like an animal.

"Kasya dosho na vidyate." — There is no person without faults.

On the importance of learning from youth:
"Learn from the mistakes of others. You can't live long enough to make them all yourself." This is the foundation of Chanakya's philosophy — study history, study human nature, study yourself.

"A man is great by deeds, not by birth."

"As long as your body is healthy and under control and death is distant, try to save your soul; when death is imminent what can you do?"

CHANAKYA NITI — BOOK 4: ON FRIENDSHIP AND ENEMIES

"Before you start some work, always ask yourself three questions: Why am I doing it? What might be the results? Will I be successful? Only when you think deeply and find satisfactory answers to these questions, go ahead."

"He who is overly attached to his family members experiences fear and sorrow, for the root of all grief is attachment. Thus one should discard attachment to be happy."

"A person should not be too honest. Straight trees are cut first and honest people are screwed first."

On identifying true friends:
"He is a wise man who does not grieve for the things which he has not, but rejoices for those which he has."

CHANAKYA NITI — BOOK 5: ON STRATEGY AND POWER

"The world's biggest power is the youth and beauty of a woman." — This refers to the power of attraction, persuasion, and social influence in negotiations.

"Never make friends with people who are above or below you in status. Such friendships will never give you any happiness."

"Test a servant while in the discharge of his duty, a relative in difficulty, a friend in adversity, and a wife in misfortune."

On the four methods of diplomacy (Sama, Dana, Bheda, Danda):
1. SAMA — Negotiation and persuasion through kindness and reason
2. DANA — Conciliation through gifts, incentives, and offerings
3. BHEDA — Division and creating splits in the enemy's camp
4. DANDA — Force and punishment as last resort

"A man is born alone and dies alone. He experiences the good and bad consequences of his karma alone, and he goes alone to hell or the Supreme abode."

ARTHASHASTRA — ON STATECRAFT AND ECONOMICS

On the seven pillars of the state (Saptanga):
1. Swami — The sovereign/leader
2. Amatya — The ministers/team
3. Janapada — The territory/market
4. Durga — The fort/infrastructure
5. Kosha — The treasury/finances
6. Danda — The army/enforcement
7. Mitra — The allies

"The root of happiness is virtue; the root of virtue is revenue; the root of revenue is proper administration; the root of proper administration is the efficient use of self and others."

On financial wisdom:
"Accumulated wealth is saved by spending just as incoming flood water is saved by letting out stagnant water."

"A debt should be repaid to a creditor, and even a small help should not go unacknowledged."

CHANAKYA ON LEADERSHIP:

"The king shall consider as good, not what pleases himself, but what pleases his subjects."

"An administrator who does not look after his people is like a shepherd who has abandoned his flock."

On time management:
"Time perfects men as well as destroys them." — Every moment unused is a resource wasted.

"Purity of speech, of the mind, of the senses, and of a compassionate heart are needed by one who desires to rise to the divine platform."

CHANAKYA ON ENEMIES:

"Even if a snake is not poisonous, it should pretend to be venomous." — Perception is power.

"Once you start a working on something, don't be afraid of failure and don't abandon it. People who work sincerely are the happiest."

"The biggest guru-mantra is: Never share your secrets with anybody. It will destroy you."

On dealing with difficult people:
"There are three gems upon this earth: food, water, and pleasing words — fools consider pieces of rocks as gems."

CHANAKYA'S PERSONAL PHILOSOPHY:

Chanakya was born in poverty, humiliated by the Nanda king, and vowed to create a new empire. He found a young boy named Chandragupta pulling out grass by its roots — interpreting this as attacking the problem at its root. He trained this boy for years and together they built the greatest empire India had ever seen.

This story illustrates Chanakya's core teaching: identify the ROOT problem, not the symptoms. Find the right person for the right task. Build systems, not dependencies. Execute without hesitation.
